# Making Things Hard on Yourself, But in a Good Way: Creating Desirable Difficulties to Enhance Learning

*Elizabeth L. Bjork & Robert A. Bjork (2011), summary edition for cookbook demo purposes.*

## Abstract

Conditions of instruction that make learning feel easy and rapid often fail
to support long-term retention and transfer, whereas conditions that
introduce difficulties for the learner — slowing apparent progress — can
enhance durable learning. We call these "desirable difficulties." This
chapter reviews four families of desirable difficulties: spacing study
sessions, interleaving topics, varying conditions of practice, and using
tests rather than presentations as learning events.

## The Storage / Retrieval Strength Distinction

Memories have two strengths. *Storage strength* reflects how well learned an
item is; it accumulates and, by hypothesis, never decreases. *Retrieval
strength* reflects current ease of access and fades with time and
interference. Critically, current performance reflects retrieval strength,
while durable learning depends on gains in storage strength — and gains in
storage strength are largest when retrieval strength is low at the time of
practice. This is why practice that feels effortful (because access is
currently hard) produces more learning than practice that feels fluent.

## Spacing versus Massing

Massed practice (cramming) produces strong short-term performance and is
therefore seductive, but spaced practice reliably wins on delayed tests. In
one classic finding, participants who studied items in spaced sessions
recalled roughly twice as much after a week as those who massed the same
total study time. The benefit grows with the retention interval.

## The Testing Effect

Retrieving information from memory is a more potent learning event than
restudying it. Tests are usually treated as assessments, but a test that a
learner attempts — even unsuccessfully, if followed by feedback — modifies
memory more than an equivalent period of re-reading. Learners consistently
mispredict this: they judge re-reading as more effective because it feels
more fluent.

## Interleaving

Blocking practice by topic (AAABBBCCC) feels organized and produces fast
within-session gains; interleaving topics (ABCABCABC) feels confusing but
produces better discrimination between concepts and better performance at a
delay. In a motor-learning and category-induction studies alike, the
majority of participants performed better after interleaving yet *believed*
blocking had worked better — a metacognitive error with direct consequences
for how students choose to study.

## Caveats

Difficulties are desirable only when the learner can overcome them: a
difficulty that exceeds the learner's background knowledge or skills becomes
undesirable. The same manipulation can therefore be desirable for an
advanced student and counterproductive for a novice.

## Implication

Learners and instructors should distrust fluency as an index of learning and
deliberately introduce spacing, interleaving, variation, and retrieval
practice — while calibrating difficulty to the learner's current state.
